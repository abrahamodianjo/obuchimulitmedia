Mad Persons, Danfo Drivers and Lagos Streets Fights

Buchi Odianjo

11 Jun, 2019

37.2k Views

It was my first day at work as an MSc Graduate Trainee with the X3M Group Lagos, it was pretty exciting and easy going to work because my Cousin had ordered an Uber for me, but heading back home was hectic as I had to jump from one danfo to the other. It was 4:35 PM, the peak of rush hour. That very day, I stood for over 20 minutes for a bus because every time I tried getting into one, every man and woman around me would hurriedly get in before me, some even pushed me out of the way just to get in. ‘Choi! No shakara for streets oh!.’

10 minutes passed, and it kept happening, “I really have to leave here!” I murmured to myself. As soon as I saw the next danfo bus approaching, no one had to tell me what to do, I chased after the bus like my life depended on it, lol! I finally got a bus heading to Ojota. While in traffic, I noticed a Man who looked mentally ill talking to himself, and something strange happened.

Out of nowhere, the Man used what he was holding to hit the wheel screen of the bus; our driver immediately swerved to his corner and almost ran over the supposed ‘mad man.’ The driver was so infuriated, got down and gave him a beating of his life; I began to scream ‘Please somebody stop the driver! Please so he doesn’t injure this man,’ to my surprise, no one said a word, not even the soldier sitting in the front seat. I kept screaming, and after like 40 seconds the soldier finally came to the rescue. The driver was so furious because the guy had broken his screen.

I realised that I was the only person screaming for mercy on the bus. I also noticed that even when the fight happened just by the road in broad daylight, no one seemed to care or park to stop the beating. I finally thought, even when this man looked mad, the bus driver didn’t even care about his state and almost beat the living daylight out of him. ‘Lagos na wa oh’ I wondered to myself. I got home and shared the story with my Cousin, and he said: “Omo nobody send for this Lagos oh, ‘oyo’ (on your own) is the case in Lagos.” But then I learnt a few lessons from this.

First of all, when it comes to jumping buses, you are not a lady or gentleman, but a hustler trying to get a bus to your destination, ‘so send nobody.’ Secondly, when you see a fight on the street, don’t be surprised if no one comes to the rescue. Again, it is not every fight that concerns you, painful to say but sometimes look away, MIND YOUR BUSINESS. And finally, not every mad-looking man on the street is as mad as a Lagos danfo driver.
